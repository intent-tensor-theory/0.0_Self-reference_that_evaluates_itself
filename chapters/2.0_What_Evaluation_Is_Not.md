# 章二・零 (Chapter 2.0 / 7.0)

## What Evaluation Is Not

**Progress: 42.9%**

---

「測ることと、意味づけることは違う。」  
*To measure is not to mean.*

「選ぶことと、評価することは違う。」  
*To choose is not to evaluate.*

---

### 2.0.1 Why Negatives Must Come First

Evaluation is one of the most abused words in foundational discourse.

It is routinely conflated with:

- measurement,
- optimization,
- reward,
- control,
- selection.

If these confusions are not eliminated first, whatever follows will be incoherent.

This chapter exists to clear the ground.

---

### 2.0.2 Evaluation Is Not Measurement

Measurement is comparison to an external scale.

A system that:

- samples a state,
- outputs a value,
- records a magnitude,

has not evaluated anything.

> Measurement answers *what is.*  
> Evaluation answers *what matters.*

A ruler does not care how long something is.  
A thermometer does not prefer one temperature over another.

**Measurement contains no preference.**

---

### 2.0.3 Evaluation Is Not Collapse

Collapse selects one outcome from many.

This can occur:

- randomly,
- deterministically,
- probabilistically.

**None of these imply evaluation.**

A die collapses to a face.  
No face is better or worse to the die.

> Selection without preference is blind.

---

### 2.0.4 Evaluation Is Not Optimization

Optimization presupposes:

- a goal,
- a cost function,
- a success criterion.

These are almost always imposed externally.

An optimizer does not decide what is valuable.  
It enforces a value already given.

Thus:

> Optimization is execution of value, not generation of value.

Evaluation must precede optimization, not follow it.

---

### 2.0.5 Evaluation Is Not Reward

Reward-based systems are frequently mistaken for evaluative systems.

A reward signal:

- reinforces behavior,
- biases future action,
- shapes trajectories.

But reward is only meaningful to the designer, not to the system, unless something further is true.

If a system cannot question, revise, or reinterpret its reward signal, then:

> the reward is not evaluated — it is obeyed.

**Obedience is not selfhood.**

---

### 2.0.6 Evaluation Is Not Control

Control systems regulate deviation from a set point.

They are:

- reactive,
- corrective,
- stabilizing.

But control does not care *why* a state is preferred.

It enforces equilibrium, not meaning.

> A control loop does not know it is controlling.

---

### 2.0.7 Evaluation Is Not Intelligence

Intelligence is performance across domains.

Evaluation is not performance.

A system may:

- solve problems,
- adapt strategies,
- outperform humans—

and still lack evaluation.

> Performance can be instrumental.  
> Evaluation is intrinsic.

---

### 2.0.8 The Common Feature of All Failures

All false notions of evaluation share one flaw:

> **The criteria come from outside the system.**

Whether it is:

- a measurement scale,
- a loss function,
- a reward signal,
- a controller,
- a programmer—

the system does not originate the preference.

**Without endogenous preference, there is no evaluation.**

---

### 2.0.9 A Sharp Exclusion

We can now state a necessary exclusion:

> **A system does not evaluate itself if removing its environment leaves its preferences unchanged.**

True evaluation is fragile.

Remove the system's internal structure, and the preference vanishes.

---

### 2.0.10 Why This Matters

Failing to distinguish evaluation from its impostors leads to:

- mistaking machines for selves,
- mistaking behavior for agency,
- mistaking complexity for meaning.

This confusion does not advance understanding.

**It blocks it.**

---

### 2.0.11 What Must Come Next

Having excluded what evaluation is not, we are now constrained.

Evaluation must be:

- internal,
- comparative,
- preference-generating,
- self-modifying.

But one gap remains.

**Comparison alone is still insufficient.**

A system can compare without caring.

Therefore, we must confront the hardest requirement directly.

---

「外から来る基準は、評価ではない。」  
*Criteria from outside are not evaluation.*
